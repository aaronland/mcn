Embedding Accessibility into Process: Lessons from a Museum Website Redesign
Wednesday October 22, 2025 1:15pm - 1:35pm CDT
Herzog Building, 5th Floor - Skyline Room
You're checked in!
Making a website accessible and inclusive for all users was a core goal of the Cleveland Museum of Art’s recent website redesign, which brought together the online collection, marketing, and e-commerce into a single platform. As the project progressed, it became clear that achieving our accessibility goals required more than compliance. It required rethinking digital processes across our institution. This expanded the project in two directions: implementing sustainable web practices and making all editorial and collection images accessible. As an art museum that relies heavily on visuals, we saw this as an opportunity to explore how AI could support our goals while navigating complexities such as accuracy and implementation.

In partnership with Prime Access Consulting (PAC), we built accessibility reviews into every stage of the redesign. These evaluations were not just technical checklists but essential moments of reflection. This work was driven by disabled experts, whose insights helped guide the team through moments of fatigue and challenge. Their behind-the-scenes labor was instrumental in ensuring accessibility was not deprioritized. Acknowledging this contribution is critical, especially in a sector where disabled labor is often invisible. Through this collaboration, we built internal capacity to identify barriers, ask better questions, and shift our long-term thinking. Accessibility became embedded in our development cycles, not added at the end.

To support these practices, we trained content editors across departments, developed style guides, created documentation, and used our Drupal backend to enforce accessible practices. By creating standardized workflows, we built a foundation that enabled content creators across the museum to meaningfully contribute to accessibility efforts.

Driven by making artwork accessible to all, we’ve also experimented with using AI to assist with alt text creation. This began with training a model using human-generated visual descriptions and reviewing its output through both automated and human assessment. Our style guide, developed with PAC, provides a reference point for assessing quality. We continue to refine our alt text, learning from the challenges of scaling AI-generated content. With over 66,000 objects in our collection, AI makes possible our goal to add alt text for all works by November 2025.

This session will cover:

- How expert guidance was integrated into ongoing development

- What it took to keep accessibility a priority under pressure

- How decentralized content workflows, training and documentation support long-term goals

- Strategies for expanding alt-text through AI and questions of accuracy and implementation that come with it

Through this project, accessibility for CMA has evolved from a deliverable to an ongoing shared commitment across our digital and content teams, supported by collaborative relationships with external partners.
