3D models and Indigenous cultural heritage from museums and beyond
Tuesday October 21, 2025 11:15am - 11:35am CDT
Herzog Building, 5th Floor - Skyline Room
You're checked in!
Museums around the world, and their visitors, have been actively creating and sharing 3D models of tangible heritage for many years. With the growth in accessible applications for scanning and photogrammetry, the quantity of 3D models of heritage has undoubtedly been increasing in recent years. However, most 3D viewing websites were never intended to host representations of cultural heritage. While the visual and interactive nature of such models may attract public attention, how can they help to raise awareness of the significance of culture heritage? Are museums doing a better job than their visitors at including contextual information for 3D models, to educate the public about heritage?

This study tracked the landscape of 3D models of cultural heritage across different online platforms, with a focus on belongings from Indigenous (First nation, Inuit, and Métis) communities within Canada. It focused on gathering information about the most popular methods of 3D model generation, analyzing metadata, recording the quantity of museum and public creators, identifying how models were being shared (i.e. view-only vs. downloadable), examining public interactivity (i.e. number of views, likes, and comments), and investigating how models were being used (i.e. institutions using them for research vs. artists integrating them into their for-profit work).

The results – which will be accessible on the Open Science Framework platform – can help museums, researchers, and Indigenous communities. For museums, it will demonstrate how their colleagues and visitors have been approaching 3D models. Observing best practices, as well as being made aware of negative practices, can help to promote more mindfulness about sharing heritage in digital spaces. It will also allow them to understand issues related to the digital appropriation of Indigenous heritage, and ways to better engage with the public through 3D models. For researchers, the results will help to understand trends in the creation such models, which is important when thinking about issues such as data sovereignty. While instances of repatriation of tangible cultural belongings are on the rise, digital colonialism remains a recognized problem. Examples of studies such as this one could be used by researchers working to build awareness about the impacts of digitizing heritage without permissions of Indigenous communities.

Finally, for Indigenous communities, the results can help identify the locations of digitized heritage and perhaps also the location of tangible heritage. This may help to facilitate future repatriation requests. Not all Indigenous communities have access to reliable internet services, so it is important to be aware that this research may not currently be useful to them. But archiving what is currently out there may be important in the future when there is (hopefully) less of a digital barrier.
