Museums, AI, and Open Systems: Trust, Autonomy and Sustainability
Tuesday October 21, 2025 2:15pm - 3:00pm CDT
Herzog Building, 3rd Floor - McGuire Theater
You're checked in!
As AI and ML reshape sectors worldwide, museums are increasingly integrating these technologies to enhance visitor experiences, streamline operations, and manage vast collections. However, an over-reliance on proprietary, commercially-driven AI and ML systems may inadvertently restrict museums, compromising both their independence and public trust. This proposal examines the pressing need for museums to consider open-source AI and ML solutions as a means of preserving transparency, control, and ethical integrity within the sector. By fostering the development of open alternatives, museums can establish themselves as custodians not only of cultural artifacts but of responsible, trusted technological stewardship.

Commercial AI platforms, typically developed by major tech companies, often come with proprietary limitations, lack of transparency, and a narrow focus that may not align with museums' unique missions. These systems may inhibit customization, constrain data management practices, and limit institutions’ flexibility to address emerging needs. For museums, which prioritize accessibility, preservation, and public accountability, the black-box nature of many commercial AI solutions poses ethical and practical challenges. Should museums uncritically adopt these systems, they risk alienating audiences concerned about data privacy, misrepresentation, and bias.

Alternatively, investing in open-source AI and ML solutions provides museums with greater flexibility and autonomy. Open systems can be tailored to align with institutional values, making them better suited to address the diverse needs of global audiences while preserving privacy and ethical considerations. Moreover, open-source technologies enable collaboration across institutions, facilitating shared innovation and cost-efficiency—essential factors in an often resource-constrained sector. By pooling resources to advance open AI systems, museums can develop sector-specific solutions that foster public trust and meet ethical standards.

By taking an active role in the development and implementation of open AI systems, museums can build trust with their audiences and reinforce their commitment to ethical technology use. This session aims to foster a roadmap for integrating open-source AI into museum practices, emphasizing the need for collective investment in non-commercial AI tools that reflect the sector’s values.

Ultimately, prioritizing open systems is not merely a technical choice but a statement of trust and commitment. Museums must lead by example, demonstrating a future where technology serves the public good, respects privacy, and aligns with the principles of inclusivity and transparency. This session will inspire the museum sector to actively engage in shaping a responsible AI landscape, ensuring that these vital cultural institutions retain their role as trusted custodians in the digital age.
