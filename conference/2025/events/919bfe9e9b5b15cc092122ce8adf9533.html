From Seed to Tree: Building AI Systems That Actually Work in Museums
Wednesday October 22, 2025 10:15am - 10:35am CDT
Herzog Building, 3rd Floor - McGuire Theater
You're checked in!
Moving beyond AI hype and vendor demonstrations, this session shares the real-world journey of building MAPLE (Morton Arboretum Plant Learning Engine) — a RAG-based AI system that helps staff respond to visitor plant identification queries. Rather than a polished success story, this is an honest look at the messy realities of implementing AI in cultural institutions: wrestling with 20 years of inconsistent data formats, balancing technical capabilities with staff workflow needs, figuring out how to define success, and creating systems that enhance rather than replace human expertise.

MAPLE processes thousands of documents from multiple sources — historical email exchanges, plant database entries, website content, and clinic resources — using open-source tools. The system provides both formatted email responses for staff use and detailed reasoning for transparency, while tracking performance metrics including response times, token usage, and associated costs.

Session Structure:

Opening: Overview of vector databases, RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation) vs. fine-tuning approaches, and why RAG often makes more sense for museums

Deep Dive: Live walkthrough of MAPLE's architecture, including  unglamorous data preparation work and how we learned to evaluate AI responses for accuracy and institutional voice

Maintenance Reality: Review of ongoing costs (actual numbers), the process for future updates, and lessons learned from six months of operational use

Interactive Demo: Attendees can test MAPLE with their own queries and examine how it handles edge cases and failures

Q&A and Framework Sharing: Decision-making tools for evaluating whether AI makes sense for your institutional challenges

Attendees will leave with practical resources including evaluation rubrics, cost planning templates, and a decision framework worksheet. This isn't about cutting-edge technology — it's about practical implementations using accessible tools that can run on modest hardware budgets and actually integrate with existing museum workflows.
