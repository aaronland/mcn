IIIF Implementations, Approaches, and Outcomes: A Common Framework for Many Stories
Wednesday October 22, 2025 1:15pm - 2:00pm CDT
Barnes Building, 8th Floor - Garden Terrace Room
You're checked in!
This session will showcase various implementations of the International Image Interoperability Framework (IIIF) to create rich digital interpretive experiences. The session intent is to exemplify a breadth of user experiences created with IIIF tools (e.g. deep zoom, annotation, pagination), highlight implementation approaches and extensions to these IIIF features, and evaluate ideal audience experiences and outcomes. Projects for three institutions will be presented – the North Carolina Museum of Art, the Workers Arts & Heritage Centre, and Getty, with speakers from project partners Urban Insight and Surface Impression.

Urban Insight and the NC Museum of Art present an annotated image viewer built for both web and in-gallery kiosk experience. Technical details include: conformance to the IIIF Presentation API within a WordPress plugin where content editors have control of annotation placement, text, and additional imagery via CMS, extension of the Clover IIIF viewer to support annotations, kiosk feature availability via query parameter and webpage embeddable via , and Google Tag Manager custom events track kiosk usage in addition to standard web analytics. This project emphasizes reusability – building once and deploying across multiple contexts – for maximizing impact when resources are limited.

Surface Impression presents a virtual exhibition website, All Together Now! Banners of the Labour Movement, developed for the Workers Arts & Heritage Centre (Canada’s only labour history museum). The featured historical banners span up to six feet wide, are usually double-sided, and reveal key historical details through wear, damage, or their alteration over time — these complexities shaped our approach to digital storytelling and user experience, all within the IIIF standard. We’ll share how we enabled virtual flipping of double-sided banners in 3D space and created custom annotations with a kiosk-like interface. The result is an enriched interpretive resource that amplifies the history of Canada’s labour movement and its messages of common purpose, pride, unity and the dignity of work.

Getty presents a digital re-creation of the Bayard Album, one of the earliest photo albums ever created, related to the exhibition Hippolyte Bayard: A Persistent Pioneer. For conservation reasons the book no longer holds the fragile photographs. Thus, this digital interactive—a re-creation of the album with interpretation, presented in-gallery—offered visitors a unique opportunity: a chance to experience this historic object in its full richness, approximating how its creator and subsequent owners may have engaged with it before the album was later disassembled for preservation. The interactive allowed a “browsing” as well as a “guided experience,” enabling users to choose. Creating the digital album leveraged the expertise of diverse groups of Getty staff and marked the first time we produced a project merging IIIF technology and content interpretation.
