Exploring Collections in Latent Space: Machine Learning for Visual Drift, Interpolation, and Insight
Tuesday October 21, 2025 2:15pm - 2:35pm CDT
Barnes Building, 8th Floor - Garden Terrace Room
You're checked in!
Latent space modeling offers a new way to engage with collections—not as rows in a database, but as fluid visual systems where relationships between objects can be explored, interpolated, and reimagined. This presentation introduces a framework for training unsupervised machine learning models—particularly VAEs and VQGANs—on small, high-resolution image datasets drawn from scientific and cultural collections. By embedding objects in a continuous visual space, we gain the ability to generate new representations that exist between known examples, opening up insights into both natural morphology and aesthetic variation.

The work began with Hawaiian seed imagery, training species-specific VAEs that could interpolate within narrowly scoped datasets. These models were deliberately limited in scope—trained without CLIP or text-based embeddings—to focus purely on visual properties: symmetry, texture, contour, noise. This constraint revealed a surprising strength: the models began to surface latent structures in the data that reflected phylogenetic relationships, collection anomalies, and in some cases, artistic potential. In short, the model didn’t need to “know” what it was looking at to find meaningful continuity.

The presentation covers both the technical workflow and conceptual implications.

Beyond botany, the same workflow has been applied to objects from archaeological, ethnographic, and artistic collections. When trained on carved forms, textiles, or repeated motifs, latent space models offer an alternative mode of formal analysis—surfacing unexpected connections across geography, culture, or time. These connections are not prescriptive; rather, they open up curatorial and scholarly questions. Why do two distinct artifacts interpolate so cleanly? Why do others resist? What might a “missing” object in the middle of that transition look like?

For curators and conservators, this technique offers a speculative framework for analyzing fragmentary or context-poor materials. For scientists, it provides a new mode of visual hypothesis testing. And for artists or exhibition designers, it’s a tool for generative creation and interpretive storytelling.

This talk is intended for those at the intersection of machine learning, visual collections, and public knowledge infrastructure. Attendees will leave with reproducible workflows, model references, and a broader perspective on how unsupervised image models can support—and challenge—the ways we interpret material culture and natural history.
