You Can't Plan Your Way Out of Complexity
Wednesday October 22, 2025 10:15am - 11:00am CDT
Herzog Building, 5th Floor - Skyline Room
You're checked in!
You’ve heard it before and maybe you’ve even said it. But still, museum technologists are handed project after project with timelines, roadmaps, and expectations that treat every problem like it’s simple or solvable by better planning. We write documentation. We build beautiful decks. We upgrade the system. And yet, nothing really changes except maybe the burnout levels of the people tasked with “implementation.”

This session is for anyone who’s ever been asked to “just make it work” when the real issue was communication breakdowns, trust gaps, decision gridlock, or invisible power dynamics. It’s for those who are tired of solving the wrong problem—or solving the right one with the wrong tools. And it’s for people who suspect there might be a better way to name, frame, and move through the real challenges of museum work today.

We’ll explore a simple but powerful framework for understanding four distinct types of problems—simple, complicated, complex, and chaotic—and why trying to solve all of them the same way doesn’t just fail… it often makes things worse. This model helps shift us from asking “How do we fix it?” to asking “What kind of problem is this, and how should we respond?” It’s an invitation to stop overengineering our approaches to human messes and start choosing methods that match the moment.

We’ll walk through real-world examples from museum teams and then jump into group discussions where participants will sort challenges from their own work lives, exchange advice, and practice right-sizing solutions. Expect micro-consulting moments, pattern recognition, and at least one sticker that says “it is what it is.”

Whether you’re a project manager, implementer, technologist, or anyone caught between configuration and people, this session will offer you:

You’ll leave with greater clarity about the work ahead, relief that you’re not the only one struggling, energy to try something different, and a few much-needed a-ha moments about why some things haven’t worked—and what might.

Because in the end?

You can’t plan your way out of complexity.

But you can learn to navigate it, together.
