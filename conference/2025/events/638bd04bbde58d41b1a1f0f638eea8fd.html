War in Court – bringing War Archives to the Web
Tuesday October 21, 2025 11:15am - 12:00pm CDT
Herzog Building, 3rd Floor - McGuire Theater
You're checked in!
War in Court is a Dutch collaborative project of four Institutes:

The National Archives; NIOD Institute for War, Holocaust and Genocide Studies; Huygens Institute for Dutch History and Culture and WO2Net.

The ambition of the project is to make the Central Archive of Special Jurisdiction (CABR) broadly accessible online. The CABR is the largest and most frequently consulted war archive in the Netherlands. The CABR is a legal archive containing files of individuals suspected of collaborating with the German occupiers during World War II. It includes 485,000 cases of approximately 425,000 people who were investigated within the Special Jurisdiction. This archive also holds information on victims, resistance activities, events, and more. The archive consists of over 30 million pages.

The archive contains legal, factual information, but is also divers, unorganized and with a lot of legal technical terms. And on top, it contains many eye witness accounts that require additional context for better understanding. The main audiences of the website (Oorlogvoorderechter.nl) are relatives: descendants of persons investigated or mentioned (as witness or victim) in the archive, so the website is built for a very large, general audience with little knowledge of archives or legal procedures. In this project a radically different way of presenting archival material was created to make the archive accessible for everyone.

The entire four kilometers of archive are digitized, mechanically transcribed, classified, digitally organized, annotated and presented on an easy-to-use website. Each page is or will be digitized (so far over 12 million pages have been processed, the entire archive will be digitized in 2027). Each scan is made machine readable (Automatic Text Recognition) based on Machine Learning/LLMs/Generative AI. Software for document classification and separation was created to reconstruct the original documents from individual scans and analyse the document to determine the type of document. Document classification is used to explain all the different types of documents in this legal archive, and it is also used to organize files in a user-friendly way: to reconstruct court procedures.

To help people understand the documents we also use Named Entity Recognition (NER): generic NER to detect people, places, organizations etcetera. The NER then is matched with a WW2 Thesaurus: a specialized encyclopedia of WW2 themes (Nazi-organizations, resistance groups, camps, war events etcetera) to explain these concepts.

In this presentation we will demonstrate the website War in Court and the different techniques to make this complicated archive accessible for an emotionally involved, general audience.

With this project we hope to facilitate the debates about good, bad and all 50 shades in between: the choices people made and the consequences of these choices.

And we also hope to improve the way Archives and Museums present their archival inventories online.
