A Taste of Our Own Medicine: Building Products for Museum Staff at MoMA
Wednesday October 22, 2025 11:15am - 11:35am CDT
Barnes Building, 8th Floor - Garden Terrace Room
You're checked in!
Digital teams at museums work on a wide variety of public-facing projects. Equally important, though, are the digital products and infrastructure projects we build to support the needs of museum staff. This work requires us to harmonize diverse functions that include collection specialists, floor staff, researchers and archivists, editorial teams, marketing departments, and more, each with their distinct goals and workstreams. Successful museum systems can handle the complex, nuanced requirements of each stakeholder and function in a way that maximizes simplicity and ease of use for everyone. When done well, technology can empower our colleagues instead of encumbering them in complicated workflows. In a context where the possibilities of digital technology are taking vast leaps while staffing resources (at best) stand still, successful digital systems can make outsized contributions towards helping museums meet their mission of caring for their visitors, showcasing art, and supporting scholarship.

As part of the process of migrating to a new content management system (CMS) and other internal projects, the team at MoMA recently conducted a series of museum-wide stakeholder interviews to understand the workflows and pain points of various departments. The learnings were numerous. There was a lot of manual duplication of content needed to bridge different systems that teams were using. Staff faced lengthy, often outdated documentation, and opaque interfaces where functionalities and outcomes weren’t clear. Several features across products relied on manual scheduling and content production, which increased workloads.

Many of these learnings found their way into projects that followed: we created a revamped website dashboard in our new CMS, which helps staff find and manage content they’re working on more easily. We optimized content creation interfaces and language to make workflows smoother, minimizing the need to depend on external documentation. Significant additions were also made to our suite of behind-the-scenes products such as an app to manage virtual queueing in the museum, ticket and membership sales management, and onsite kiosk management. And lastly, amidst all this work, the team also snuck in a few tools and features to make life easier for itself: for example, a Figma plugin that allows designers to quickly pull accurate artwork, artist, and exhibition data into their designs.

In this presentation, we’ll talk about the process of turning the researcher's lens on ourselves: how we approached, conducted, and synthesized our conversations with staff, converted those into actionable ideas and opportunities, and tested in-flight ideas to make sure we were headed in the right direction. We’ll also discuss the impact we’ve seen and learnings we’ve gathered following the release of those initiatives. Finally, we’ll go over some other ideas we’d love to work on in the future that might be relevant to the audience.
