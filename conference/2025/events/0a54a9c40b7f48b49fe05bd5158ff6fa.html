3D Digital Assets & Longevity: Data Creation, Maintenance, and Preservation Strategies
Wednesday October 22, 2025 11:15am - 12:00pm CDT
Herzog Building, 5th Floor - Skyline Room
You're checked in!
3D digitization programs have high potential to transform conservation, research, education, and engagement in museums. Conservators can plan restorations and preserve fragile objects without handling if detailed digital records are captured, reducing risk of damage. Museum educators are also increasingly exploring the potential of 3D digitizations and 3D prints to enhance engagement and accessibility – creating immersive extended reality experiences and tactile programming with artifact replicas.

However, the technical complexity of producing and maintaining 3D digital derivatives presents significant challenges to the sustainability of 3D work. Various techniques are available for 3D digitization and 3D printing, and several competing standards exist in file formats for 3D models. Staff from two institutions, the North Carolina Museum of Art and the Williams College Museum of Art, will present ongoing work toward creating standard procedures for 3D assets work to ensure longevity beyond turnover of key personnel. 

The NC Museum of Art’s 3D digitization program began as an initiative within the education department, with nearly all digitization work carried out by external contractors. Collections and IT staff did not oversee the deliverables, leading to inconsistencies in 3D data specifications and risk of final models never making it to staff custody. The presenters are working to reframe 3D digitization as a long-term investment in the NCMA collection. Digitization is costly and we aim for 3D models to endure. We will discuss how contracts are drafted to specify deliverables and how we verify, archive, and publish finished digital assets.

At the Williams College Museum of Art, 3D model making began with needs of college-level classes ranging from Anthropology and American Studies to Chemistry. With support from prep staff, most work is carried out by one staff member, and the museum has no devoted IT support. Many models are created by students as part of internships or classes. Our process developed from the need to balance external access to high-resolution models and source files with the constraint of mandated storage and access systems like Google Drive. This approach is suitable to organizations trying to develop a sustainable program with student or volunteer labor and minimal system customization. 

We will present our use cases, the educational programs and research tools around 3D collection objects, especially as project goals dictate specifications for mesh and texture optimizations as well as file types. However, our primary focus will be on policies, procedures, and long-term strategies for 3D data creation and maintenance.  To offer a broad scope of perspectives on sustaining 3D asset initiatives this presentation brings together staff from digital imaging, information technology, and education at a mid-sized museum, in addition to curatorial & teaching perspective at a college museum supported by external IT.
