Classifying Emails with AI for Fun (and Non-Profit)
Wednesday October 22, 2025 2:15pm - 2:35pm CDT
Herzog Building, 5th Floor - Skyline Room
You're checked in!
Picture this: Visitor emails continually flood your institution’s inboxes. Admission questions are mixed with emergency accessibility needs and international tourism inquiries arrive alongside local school group bookings. Each requires different expertise, response times, and cultural sensitivity. Your staff feels overwhelmed, visitors wait too long for replies, and essential communications risk getting lost in the shuffle.

As museums strive to serve diverse audiences, email communication remains a critical but tedious touchpoint. Email inbox rules are set up to help, but become outdated and are hard to maintain. Tending to a catch-all inbox, even after the spam filter has done its job, is a time-consuming job that is never done.

This case study presents our prototype solution: an AI system that classifies incoming emails along multiple dimensions, to route to the right team members with subject matter expertise, to ensure fair and timely responses.

Built on an open-source DistilBERT transformer (an AI model) with custom architecture for museum contexts, the system identifies the nuanced needs of different visitor segments—from international tourists requiring language assistance to local community members seeking accessibility accommodations.

How It Works: Our prototype simultaneously analyzes emails across three(-ish) dimensions—category, urgency, and visitor segment. A hierarchical category-subcategory structure captures nuanced inquiries (booking -> accessibility accommodation.) The resulting classifications are used to get every email to the right person to respond to it.

The system is private: no visitor emails are fed into ChatGPT, Gemini or any other big cloud AI. Since DistilBERT is quite small and efficient, the system runs on modest resources. 

This presentation will glance at the technical architecture and demonstrate how museums can extend the system for their specific taxonomies and use cases. The authors will share personal experiences with using AI tools like Claude, Gemini and Cursor to build an AI solution.
